Introduction
Technology has become a key indicator of a nation's economic development — countries either drive progress by producing it or depend on it by consuming imports. This report examines whether Egypt falls into the consumer or producer category using secondary data from the World Bank (1960–2023) across 17 ICT and economic indicators.
Research Question
Does Egypt function primarily as a consumer of technology, a producer of technology, or does the quantitative evidence suggest a transitional or mixed position?

Productivity ↔ Distraction (r = −0.06): Near-zero correlation, suggesting distraction score alone is not a strong linear predictor of productivity in this sample.

Key Findings
1 Strong positive correlation (r=0.86) between internet penetration and high-tech exports — as digital access grew, exports rose significantly.
2 Egypt is a confirmed net digital importer. Service imports (35.8%) far exceed exports (20.0%) — highly significant (t=−13.09).
3 Mobile subscription growth positively linked to services sector GDP expansion (r=0.64) — digital connectivity supports service-sector growth.
4 Internet explains ~30% of manufacturing GDP variance with a negative coefficient — consistent with a structural shift toward services.

Conclusion
Egypt has successfully built a massive digital market but hasn't become a tech maker yet. People are online, services are growing, but the country buys twice as much tech from abroad as it sells.

The biggest opportunity: Egypt has the audience and the mobile infrastructure — the challenge now is transitioning from user to creator.
Potential Issues
Sample size: H₁ uses only 17 observations (2008–2024).
Causality: All analyses are correlational only — no causal direction confirmed.
Structural breaks: 2011 Revolution, 2016 devaluation, COVID-19 not modeled.
Aggregation: National data masks important regional disparities.

Overall Verdict
Egypt University of Informatics · Data Analysis Course · 2024
Egypt is currently a technology consumer, not a producer. While mobile and internet adoption have soared, the ICT trade deficit persists across both goods (3× imports over exports) and services (35.8% vs 20%). Digital adoption is reshaping the economy toward services — the opportunity is to transition from user to creator.
